## Scenario A: Cash Bonus → Personal Purchase

### Structure
1. Company pays Matty a cash bonus sufficient to purchase the car after taxes
2. Matty buys the car personally
3. Matty owns the car outright from day one

### Tax Implications

**To net $230K after taxes, Matty needs gross bonus of ~$380K:**

| Tax Component | Rate | Amount |
|---------------|------|--------|
| Federal Income Tax | 37% | ~$140,000 |
| Medicare (Employee) | 2.35% | ~$8,900 |
| Social Security | 6.2% | $0 (if over wage base) |
| Florida State | 0% | $0 |
| **Total Tax to Matty** | | **~$150,000** |

**Company Side:**
| Tax Component | Rate | Amount |
|---------------|------|--------|
| Employer Medicare | 1.45% | ~$5,500 |
| Employer SS | 6.2% | $0 (if over wage base) |
| **Total Additional Cost** | | **~$6,000** |

**Total Cost to Company:** ~$386,000
**Company Deduction:** $386,000

### Pros
- Cleanest structure
- Matty owns car immediately
- No transfer complexity
- Clear paper trail

### Cons
- Highest tax burden
- Company needs $386K cash to give Matty a $230K car
- Not practical for a pre-revenue company

### Verdict: ❌ Not Recommended
Requires too much cash and highest tax inefficiency.

## Scenario B: Company Owns Car → Transfer at Milestone

### Structure
1. Company (C-Corp) purchases the car
2. Matty has personal use of the car
3. At milestone (1 yr OR $1M revenue), title transfers to Matty
4. Transfer value = taxable compensation

### Tax Implications

**During Use Period:**
- Personal use of company vehicle = taxable fringe benefit
- Annual Lease Value (ALV) for $230K car ≈ $45,000-50,000/year
- Matty pays tax on this "phantom income" each year

**At Transfer:**
- FMV at transfer date = W-2 compensation
- GT3 likely still worth $200,000-230,000
- Tax on transfer: ~$75,000-85,000

**Potential Double Taxation Problem:**
- Tax on personal use: ~$15,000-18,000/year
- Tax on transfer: ~$75,000-85,000
- **Total could exceed car's value**

### Company Considerations
- Luxury auto depreciation limits apply (~$12,200 year 1, then declining)
- Full depreciation would take 20+ years
- Company gets compensation deduction at transfer

### Pros
- Company asset on books
- Deferred taxation until transfer
- Company gets deduction

### Cons
- Complex accounting
- Personal use creates annual taxable income
- Potential for total tax > car value
- C-Corp double taxation risk if characterized as dividend

### Verdict: ❌ Not Recommended
Too complex and potential for over-taxation.

## Scenario C: Personal Co-Ownership (Treated as Compensation)

### Structure
1. Alex purchases the car personally
2. Alex and Matty co-own (either joint title or via personal LLC)
3. At milestone, Alex transfers his portion to Matty
4. Transfer is treated as compensation for services

### Option C1: Simple Joint Title

**Setup:**
- Both names on car title (50/50 or other split)
- Informal agreement on usage, insurance, maintenance
- At milestone, Alex signs over his portion

**Tax at Transfer:**
| Component | Amount |
|-----------|--------|
| FMV of Alex's portion | ~$115,000-230,000 |
| Federal tax (37%) | ~$43,000-85,000 |
| Medicare (2.35%) | ~$2,700-5,400 |
| **Total Tax to Matty** | **~$45,000-90,000** |

### Option C2: Personal LLC Holding the Car

**Setup:**
1. Form Florida LLC (~$125, 30 minutes online)
2. Alex contributes $230K, receives 100% membership interest
3. LLC purchases the car
4. Operating agreement specifies: at milestone, Alex's interest transfers to Matty
5. Matty ends up with 100% of LLC (and car)

**Benefits of LLC:**
- Liability protection (accidents hit LLC, not personal assets)
- Clean documentation
- Clear transfer mechanism
- Operating agreement governs all terms

**Tax Treatment:**
Same as C1 - transfer is compensation, Matty pays income tax on FMV.

### Pros
- Simpler than corporate ownership
- Clear liability protection (LLC option)
- Alex can potentially deduct as business expense if structured properly
- Keeps corporate books clean

### Cons
- Still ~$80K tax to Matty
- Transfer still triggers compensation treatment due to milestone language

### Verdict: ⚠️ Acceptable
Works if compensation treatment is acceptable. LLC version recommended for liability protection.

## Scenario D: Personal Gift (Recommended)

### Structure
1. Alex purchases the car personally
2. Alex gifts the car to Matty outright (no conditions, no milestones)
3. Gift is completely separate from any company arrangements

### Tax Implications

**To Matty:**
| Component | Amount |
|-----------|--------|
| Income Tax | **$0** |
| Other Tax | **$0** |

Gifts are not taxable income to the recipient.

**To Alex:**
| Component | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Gift Tax Return | Form 709 required |
| Gift Tax Owed | $0 (uses lifetime exemption) |
| Lifetime Exemption Used | $230,000 of $13,610,000 |
| Remaining Exemption | $13,380,000 |
| Deduction | None |

### Requirements for Gift Treatment
- **No conditions or milestones** tied to the gift
- **No performance requirements**
- **Not documented in any company agreement** as compensation
- Genuine gift intent (Alex wants to give Matty a car)

### Risk Factors
- If IRS sees milestone language in any agreement, may recharacterize as compensation
- If clearly tied to "building the platform," looks like compensation
- Need clean separation from company arrangements

### Optional: LLC Structure for Liability Protection

Even with the gift approach, you can add liability protection:

1. Alex forms Florida LLC
2. LLC purchases the car (Alex contributes $230K)
3. Alex gifts 100% membership interest to Matty
4. Matty owns LLC (and car) with liability shield

**Benefits:**
- Same $0 tax treatment
- Accidents/claims hit LLC, not personal assets
- Clean ownership documentation

### Pros
- **$0 tax to Matty**
- Simple structure
- No corporate involvement
- Alex has plenty of exemption to use
- Can add LLC for liability protection

### Cons
- Alex gets no deduction
- Must be genuinely structured as gift (no milestones)
- Some IRS recharacterization risk
- Uses portion of Alex's gift tax exemption

### Verdict: ✅ Strongly Recommended
Best tax outcome with proper structuring. Saves ~$80-90K compared to compensation treatment.
